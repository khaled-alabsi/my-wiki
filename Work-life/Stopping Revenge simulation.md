The sources highlight several "golden rules" and practical principles for distinguishing between healthy preparation and harmful mental rehearsal, as well as strategies for interrupting negative work simulations.

### The Primary Rule of Thumb
The most fundamental rule for identifying the nature of your thoughts is:
*   **If the thought ends in a clear task, it is probably preparation. If it ends in another imagined argument, it is probably rumination** [1].

### Core Strategic Rules
A central "practical conclusion" for managing these mental loops involves a combined process-based approach:
*   **Label the loop**: Identify it for what it is (e.g., "This is a work-threat simulation") [2, 3].
*   **Do not enter the imaginary debate**: Refuse to engage with the follow-up objections of a person who isn't there [2, 4].
*   **Identify one real action**: Determine if a concrete task exists; if so, write it down in under 60 seconds [2, 5].
*   **Anchor attention**: Return your focus to the present moment or the current task for 30 to 90 seconds [2, 5].
*   **Rehearse one neutral boundary response**: Prepare a single, calm sentence for the actual meeting rather than an escalatory retort [2].

### Rules for Cognitive Intervention
The sources provide specific "decision rules" based on the type of mental struggle you are experiencing:
*   **Switch from abstract to concrete**: Instead of "I must crush this question," ask "What is the real issue and my one-sentence answer?" [6].
*   **Allow, Label, and Redirect**: Replace the urge to "stop thinking" (which often fails) with this three-step process [7].
*   **Use the "No-Debate" Rule**: Commit to a two-minute period of no internal debate as soon as a simulation starts to stop the mental neutralising behaviour that keeps the loop alive [4, 8].
*   **Prepare for Reality, Not Combat**: Focus your preparation on decisions, evidence, and agendas rather than a colleague's personality or the need to "win" [9, 10].

### Operational Rules for Meetings
When actually in a high-pressure workplace interaction, the following tactics serve as "rules" for maintaining professionalism:
*   **Use Micro-pauses**: When you feel a surge of emotion, use a slow exhale before answering [11].
*   **Narrow the Scope**: If a question is muddy, ask which specific risk (e.g., cost or timing) is being addressed [12].
*   **Name the Process, Not the Motive**: If you feel insulted, separate the requirement being discussed from the implementation, rather than attacking the colleague's character [13, 14].
