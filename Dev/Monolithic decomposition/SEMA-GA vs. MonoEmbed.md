# SEMA-GA vs. MonoEmbed (2025) + FoSCI (2021) — Stepwise Comparison

## Framing

SEMA-GA is positioned as the hybridization of exactly these two ancestors. FoSCI contributes the *evolutionary multi-objective search skeleton* on structural fitness; MonoEmbed contributes the *LLM-based semantic representation*. Neither parent does what the other does, and SEMA-GA’s central claim is that fusing them — and adding an LLM inside the GA loop, not just before it — closes the gap. The comparison below walks the pipeline of a decomposition method end-to-end and shows, at each step, what each of the three systems does.

-----

## Step 1 — Input representation of the monolith

FoSCI ingests the monolith by running it under a tracing tool (Kieker) and collecting representative execution traces; the analysis primitive is therefore a *dynamic call relation between methods/classes observed at runtime*, organized into “functional atoms” that correspond to user-triggered scenarios. The representation is purely structural and behavioral — there is no notion of what a class *means*.

MonoEmbed takes the opposite path. It feeds raw source-code fragments of each class through a pretrained code language model (CodeBERT, GraphCodeBERT, UniXcoder, or LLM2Vec), fine-tuned with contrastive learning so that classes belonging to the same intended microservice are pulled together in vector space and classes from different services are pushed apart. The representation is therefore *semantic*: a dense vector per class that encodes what the code does, not how it calls other code.

SEMA-GA combines both. Its formal object is a typed attributed multi-graph in which every node carries (a) a role type inferred by static analysis — UI, domain, infra, API, unknown — (b) a semantic embedding from a pretrained code encoder in the MonoEmbed style, and (c) edges weighted by static frequency or dynamic traces in the FoSCI style. SEMA-GA inherits MonoEmbed’s “see well” and FoSCI’s “structural ground truth” in the same data structure.

## Step 2 — Initial candidate generation

FoSCI generates an initial population by treating each functional atom as a seed and randomly assigning atoms to service candidates; the population is therefore *uniformly random* over admissible partitions of the trace-derived atoms.

MonoEmbed has no notion of a “population.” It produces *one* candidate decomposition by clustering the embedding matrix once (typically with HDBSCAN or k-means). There is no second candidate to compare against.

SEMA-GA does both at once. Its initial population is seeded by a three-way mixture: a fraction is uniformly random (FoSCI-style diversity), a fraction is produced by running MonoEmbed-style clustering on the embeddings (so the GA starts no worse than the strongest single-shot method), and a fraction is generated by prompting an LLM to propose typed decompositions directly from the graph summary. This is the first place where SEMA-GA structurally subsumes both parents: MonoEmbed’s output becomes one chromosome among many, not the final answer.

## Step 3 — Variation (how new candidates are produced)

FoSCI uses classical NSGA-II operators: uniform crossover on integer-valued chromosomes (swap cluster labels position-by-position between two parents) and bit-flip mutation (randomly reassign a class to a different service). These operators are *syntactic* — they move integer labels around without any understanding of whether the resulting cluster is architecturally coherent.

MonoEmbed has no variation operator at all. It is single-shot. Once HDBSCAN returns its clustering, there is no second pass.

SEMA-GA replaces the syntactic operators with two LLM-mediated ones. Semantic crossover serializes both parent decompositions into structured natural-language summaries, prompts the LLM to recombine them while preserving architectural intent, and parses the response into a child labelling — falling back to classical uniform crossover only when the LLM output fails parsing or violates typing constraints. Reflective mutation selects an under-performing individual, shows the LLM both its serialization and its objective vector, and asks the LLM to *diagnose* the architectural pathology in one sentence and propose *one targeted edit* (e.g., move all `*Repository` classes from frontend-2 to backend-1). This is the operator that has no analogue in either parent: it is verbal-gradient reasoning, borrowed from the LLM-EA literature (EUREKA, ReEvo), grafted onto an architectural-labelling task.

## Step 4 — Fitness evaluation

FoSCI evaluates each individual on three purely numerical structural objectives — modularity, independence of functionality, and independence of evolvability — computed deterministically from the trace-derived graph. Fitness is a fixed function; there is no learned component.

MonoEmbed has no explicit fitness function per se: the clustering algorithm internally optimizes embedding distance, and quality is judged only post hoc against ground-truth decompositions using metrics like MoJoFM, precision, and recall.

SEMA-GA carries a *five-objective* vector. Four objectives are numerical and inherited essentially from the FoSCI/Mono2Micro tradition: structural modularity (SM), interface number (IFN), inter-partition calls (IPC), non-extreme distribution (NED). The fifth objective is the *LLM-as-Pareto-critic*: the LLM is prompted with the chromosome serialization and a fixed rubric, and returns a scalar in [0,1] capturing qualities the numerical metrics miss — bounded-context coherence, layering discipline, naming consistency. To suppress LLM-judge noise, three samples are drawn per individual and the median is taken. This fifth dimension is a strict Pareto criterion, not a hidden scalarization, so an individual that is numerically excellent but architecturally incoherent can be dominated by one that trades a small numerical loss for a large qualitative gain.

## Step 5 — Selection and diversity

FoSCI uses the canonical NSGA-II machinery: fast non-dominated sort to rank individuals by Pareto layer, and crowding distance to maintain spread along each layer.

MonoEmbed has no selection step.

SEMA-GA keeps the NSGA-II non-dominated sort but augments crowding distance with *semantic niching* in embedding space. Each individual is mapped to a fixed-length behavioral signature by averaging the per-node embeddings inside each component and concatenating across components; the niche penalty is the cosine similarity to the nearest archive member. The purpose is to counter LLM-induced mode collapse — a documented failure of LLM-driven search where all offspring drift toward a single semantic prior — and to keep the Pareto archive spanning genuinely different architectural strategies (e.g., domain-first vs. capability-first decompositions). This is a direct adaptation of MAP-Elites behavior characterization to a constrained Pareto setting and has no analogue in either FoSCI or MonoEmbed.

## Step 6 — Operator scheduling

FoSCI uses fixed crossover and mutation rates, set once at the start of the run.

MonoEmbed has no operators to schedule.

SEMA-GA adds an *adaptive operator scheduler*: every five generations, an LLM is shown a JSON snapshot of recent per-operator success counts, hypervolume gain, and population diversity, and recommends a new probability simplex over the operator catalogue. This recommendation is mixed with a classical multi-armed bandit’s recommendation. The role of the LLM here is to inject prior knowledge about *when* exploration (semantic crossover) versus exploitation (reflective mutation) is most likely to pay off — knowledge a credit-only bandit cannot synthesize from a few hundred observations.

## Step 7 — Output

FoSCI terminates with a Pareto front of candidate decompositions, from which the user manually selects one according to their preferred trade-off between the three structural objectives.

MonoEmbed returns exactly one decomposition — the output of the single clustering pass.

SEMA-GA terminates with a constrained Pareto archive over five objectives, optionally with explicit typing of every component as frontend, backend, or API service, and respecting an acyclicity constraint on inter-component calls. The user inspects the archive and selects according to whichever trade-off matters most for their deployment.

## Step 8 — Iteration / feedback

This is the step the paper itself names as the gap. FoSCI iterates — it is a GA, so it runs hundreds of generations — but each iteration optimizes a fixed, syntactic fitness. The search has no mechanism to *understand* why a particular decomposition is bad.

MonoEmbed does not iterate at all. One forward pass through the encoder, one clustering pass, done. If the result is wrong, there is no feedback loop.

SEMA-GA iterates *and* the iteration is informed at every step by an LLM that can read decompositions in natural language. Reflective mutation in particular operationalizes a feedback loop: the LLM sees a bad individual, diagnoses what is wrong, and proposes a fix. Over many generations this acts as a verbal gradient on the architectural quality landscape, complementing the numerical gradient implicit in NSGA-II selection.

## What SEMA-GA inherits vs. invents

From FoSCI it inherits the entire evolutionary scaffolding — NSGA-II, non-dominated sort, multi-objective Pareto framing, structural fitness proxies (SM, IFN, IPC, NED are direct descendants of Jin et al.’s modularity family). From MonoEmbed it inherits the semantic representation of code and the use of an LLM as a source of architectural meaning. What SEMA-GA invents is the *placement of the LLM inside the evolutionary loop* rather than as a one-shot pre-processor: the LLM acts as crossover, mutation, qualitative judge, scheduler, and niching kernel simultaneously. The typing of components into frontend/backend/API and the acyclicity constraint are layered on top as a deployment-relevant problem framing, but the paper itself emphasizes that the algorithmic novelty is the LLM-GA hybrid, not the typing schema.

The one-line summary: FoSCI searches well but sees poorly; MonoEmbed sees well but does not search; SEMA-GA composes them so each compensates for the other’s weakness.
